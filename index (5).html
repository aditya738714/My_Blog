Introduction

"The E85 debate looks like a fuel debate. Look closer, and it is about water, food, land, farmers, and the very direction of India's $3.7 trillion economy."

Whenever crude oil prices rise, India faces a familiar arithmetic problem. The country imports roughly 85% of the crude oil it consumes — approximately 4.7 million barrels per day. Every $10 rise in global oil prices adds nearly ₹1 lakh crore (approximately $12 billion) to India's annual import bill. That cost then ripples through transportation, inflation, household budgets, and the rupee's exchange rate.

To escape this dependence, successive Indian governments have promoted ethanol blending — mixing domestically produced ethanol from agricultural crops into petrol. The logic is straightforward: if we can grow fuel at home, we need to import less oil from abroad.

And to a significant extent, it has worked. India recently achieved 20% ethanol blending (E20) nationwide, five years ahead of its original 2030 target. The government now has ambitions to go further — to E85, where 85% of the fuel in your tank is ethanol and only 15% is petrol.

But beneath this seemingly straightforward energy policy lies a far more complex web of decisions involving water, agriculture, food security, farmer livelihoods, export economics, and the emerging question of whether India should be directing its limited natural resources toward traditional biofuels or toward twenty-first-century industries like semiconductors, data centres, and advanced manufacturing.

This is not a blog that says E85 is good or E85 is bad. It is a blog that follows the full supply chain — from the sugarcane field to the petrol pump — and asks: what are the real costs, who bears them, and what could India achieve if it made different choices with the same resources?

Chapter 01 · The Basics

What Exactly Is E85?

Before we discuss whether E85 is good or bad for India, we need to understand what it actually is. The number in the name refers to the percentage of ethanol in the fuel blend. So E20 is 20% ethanol and 80% petrol. E85 is 85% ethanol and just 15% petrol.

What is ethanol made from?

Ethanol is essentially alcohol — the same compound that makes beverages intoxicating, but produced in industrial quantities from starchy or sugary crops. When a crop like sugarcane is harvested, its juice contains sucrose. When yeast ferments this sucrose, it produces ethanol and carbon dioxide. The ethanol is then distilled and dehydrated to the purity required for fuel blending.

What is molasses?

Molasses is the thick, dark syrup that remains after sugar crystals are extracted from cane juice. It still contains significant amounts of fermentable sugar. Sugar mills in India produce both B-heavy molasses (intermediate extraction stage) and C-heavy molasses (final extraction stage). B-heavy molasses produces more ethanol per tonne, which is why the government has recently removed restrictions on its use for ethanol production.

Why is E85 a bigger jump than E20?

Going from E20 to E85 is not a linear or simple progression. It requires fundamentally different vehicles (flex-fuel engines), different storage infrastructure (stainless steel tanks to prevent corrosion), different distribution systems, and most critically — approximately 3 to 4 times more ethanol production than what India currently achieves. As of 2025, India's installed ethanol production capacity is about 1,990 crore litres per year. E20 requires roughly 1,000 crore litres. E85 at the same petrol consumption level would require over 4,200 crore litres — a gap of extraordinary magnitude.

Chapter 02 · The Hidden Cost

The Hidden Ingredient in Every Litre of Ethanol: Water

When you fill your tank with E85, you see a liquid fuel. What you do not see is the enormous quantity of water that was consumed to produce it. This is the concept of a water footprint — the total volume of water used directly and indirectly to produce a product.

"One litre of ethanol from sugarcane requires somewhere between 2,500 and 4,000 litres of water to produce. Fill a 40-litre tank with E85 and you have consumed, invisibly, up to 1.36 lakh litres of water."

Where does all this water go?

The water used in ethanol production occurs at two distinct stages, and understanding both is essential:

1

Field-level irrigation

This is the largest water consumer by far. Crops require continuous moisture throughout their growing cycle. Sugarcane, for instance, is a 12-to-18-month crop that needs water virtually year-round. In states like Maharashtra and Karnataka, where annual rainfall is around 1,000-1,200mm but sugarcane needs the equivalent of 3,000mm, farmers pump groundwater continuously.

~95% of total water footprint happens here
2

Distillery processing

Once the crop reaches the distillery, water is needed for washing, fermentation, distillation, and cooling. A 100-kilolitre-per-day ethanol plant draws approximately 400,000-450,000 litres of fresh groundwater daily. However, a significant portion of this is recycled within the plant, making the industrial water footprint much smaller than the agricultural one.

~5% of total water footprint
3

Wastewater (Vinasse)

Ethanol distilleries produce a toxic dark liquid byproduct called vinasse or spent wash. For every litre of ethanol produced, approximately 8-15 litres of vinasse are generated. This is a significant water pollution concern — vinasse can contaminate soil and groundwater if not properly treated and disposed of.

Chapter 03 · The Resource Reality

India's Groundwater Bank Account

The most useful way to understand India's water situation is to think of groundwater like money in a bank account. Every year, nature makes deposits — through monsoon rains seeping into the soil, rivers recharging aquifers, and watershed flows. Every year, humans make withdrawals — through agricultural pumping, industrial extraction, and urban water supply.

The crisis emerges when withdrawals consistently exceed deposits. The account balance shrinks year after year, and eventually the bank runs dry.

The state-by-state crisis — some regions are overdrawn

The national average masks alarming state-level realities. Punjab is perhaps the most documented case. The state receives 600-700mm of rainfall annually — semi-arid by definition. Yet it grows paddy rice that requires 1,200-1,500mm of water. The shortfall is extracted from underground aquifers. Punjab now extracts over 150 units of groundwater for every 100 units recharged — a deficit that grows larger every single year.

What does "150 units extracted per 100 recharged" actually mean?

Imagine you earn ₹1 lakh per year but spend ₹1.5 lakh. You survive by drawing down your savings. In Punjab's case, the savings are ancient fossil aquifers — water trapped underground thousands of years ago that cannot be meaningfully recharged on human timescales. Once these aquifers are depleted, the water is gone permanently unless enormous recharge programmes are implemented over decades.

In 2016, the situation in Latur district of Maharashtra became so severe that water had to be transported by train — borewells in the region had gone as deep as 90 to 250 metres to reach diminishing water tables.

How did we get here? The Green Revolution debt

India's groundwater problem has its roots in the Green Revolution of the 1960s and 1970s. When the country faced famine-level food shortages, the government promoted high-yield varieties of wheat and rice that required intensive irrigation. Borewells became the solution to reliable water supply. Simultaneously, states began offering free or heavily subsidised electricity to farmers — meaning pumping groundwater cost virtually nothing. Farmers with zero marginal cost of water have no incentive to conserve it.

This structural problem predates ethanol. But an aggressive E85 programme would dramatically accelerate it. More ethanol means more agricultural land under water-intensive crops, means more borewell pumping, means faster aquifer depletion.

"India supports 15% of the world's population but has access to only 4% of the world's freshwater resources. Agriculture already consumes 90% of all groundwater extracted. Adding large-scale ethanol crops to this system is not a small decision — it is a decision about who gets water and who does not."

Chapter 04 · The Production Process

How Ethanol Is Actually Made — Step by Step

Understanding ethanol production in detail helps explain why it is so resource-intensive, and where in the process different interventions could reduce the environmental cost.

The sugarcane route (most water-intensive)

1

Cultivation (12–18 months)

Sugarcane is planted and grown for 12 to 18 months. In irrigation-dependent states, farmers run borewell pumps nearly continuously. 1 tonne of sugarcane requires approximately 2 lakh litres of groundwater. The national average yield is 70-80 tonnes per hectare, meaning each hectare of sugarcane consumes roughly 1.4 to 1.6 crore litres of water per crop cycle.

2

Crushing and juice extraction

Harvested cane is transported to sugar mills where it is crushed to extract juice (containing 12-14% sucrose). From 1 tonne of cane, approximately 100kg of sugar is produced. The remaining juice, called B-heavy or C-heavy molasses, is used as ethanol feedstock. From 1 tonne of cane, roughly 70-80 litres of ethanol can be produced via the sugarcane juice route.

3

Fermentation (2–3 days)

Yeast is added to the juice or molasses. Over 48-72 hours at controlled temperatures, yeast converts sugars into ethanol and CO₂. The resulting "beer" contains approximately 8-12% ethanol by volume. Temperature control during fermentation requires cooling water — yet another water demand.

4

Distillation and rectification

The fermented liquid is heated in distillation columns. Since ethanol boils at 78.3°C (lower than water at 100°C), it vaporises first and is collected. Multiple distillation stages concentrate ethanol to 95% purity (rectified spirit). Steam for distillation is generated from bagasse (sugarcane fibre waste), which partially reduces the energy cost.

5

Dehydration to anhydrous ethanol

For fuel use, ethanol must reach 99.5% purity (anhydrous = without water), as any water content can cause phase separation when mixed with petrol. This dehydration uses molecular sieves or azeotropic distillation — additional energy-intensive processes. The resulting anhydrous ethanol is then denatured (made undrinkable) and transported to oil marketing company (OMC) depots.

The maize route (more efficient)

Maize (corn) has emerged as the dominant feedstock for India's grain-based ethanol, comprising 45.7% of current allocation. The process differs slightly: maize kernels are ground, cooked with water to liquefy starch, then treated with enzymes (amylase) to convert starch into fermentable sugars, before the same fermentation-distillation process follows. From 1 tonne of maize, approximately 380-400 litres of ethanol can be produced — a much higher conversion ratio than sugarcane.

Why maize is the better choice

Maize requires 900-1,800 litres of water per litre of ethanol (vs 2,500-4,000 for sugarcane). It takes 3-4 months to grow (vs 12-18 months for sugarcane). It produces more ethanol per tonne of crop. And it can be grown in more diverse regions across India, reducing geographic concentration of water stress.

However, even maize faces limits at E85 scale. The question is not which crop is best — it is whether any crop-based ethanol pathway can supply 4,200 crore litres annually without causing severe agricultural and water disruption.

Second-generation (2G) ethanol — the ideal but not yet ready

Second-generation (2G) ethanol is produced from agricultural residues — rice straw, wheat straw, sugarcane bagasse, corn cobs — that have no food value and are currently burned in fields (causing the stubble-burning pollution crisis in Punjab and Haryana). 2G ethanol has a dramatically lower water footprint since no new crop needs to be grown and it uses waste material.

India's first commercial 2G ethanol plant opened in Panipat, Haryana in 2022. However, experts note that scaling 2G to E85 volumes requires "massive infrastructure investment and technological breakthroughs." 2G ethanol currently represents a negligible fraction of India's total production.

India's ethanol supply year runs from November 1 to October 31 — aligned with the sugar crushing season. The average procurement price: is it even profitable?

For ESY 2024-25, the government set the average procurement price of ethanol at ₹71.55 per litre (inclusive of transport and GST). Notably, this is higher than the cost of refined petrol at the time of the policy announcement — meaning OMCs pay a premium for ethanol. This premium is effectively a subsidy that supports farmer incomes and rural distillery investments but adds to the overall cost of the blending programme.

The feedstock risk — what happened in 2023-24

In 2023-24, a poor sugarcane harvest combined with depleting FCI rice stocks caused a genuine feedstock shortage. India's ethanol blending rate actually dropped to 11.5% that year — a painful reminder that agricultural supply variability is a structural vulnerability in the ethanol programme. A country dependent on E85 would be extremely exposed to any single bad monsoon year.

Chapter 06 · Global Comparison

Countries That Have Adopted Ethanol — and What Happened

India is not the first country to try ethanol blending. Several nations around the world have experimented with biofuel programmes, each with different results. Studying their experiences provides critical lessons — both positive and cautionary.

  • Land-use concerns repeatedly cited as limiting factor
  • Prioritising electrification over biofuels now

🇹🇭 Thailand — Sugarcane-based

  • E20 available nationwide
  • Sugarcane-based ethanol programme since 2000s
  • Water stress in cane-growing regions documented
  • Moving toward cellulosic (2G) ethanol

What global experience tells us

Even Brazil — the world's most successful ethanol economy — has had to adjust blending mandates downward in years of poor sugarcane harvests. The US has found that E85 infrastructure remains sparse and consumer uptake is low even where available. The EU has fundamentally pivoted away from biofuels toward electrification. These are not minor footnotes — they are significant signals about the long-term trajectory of crop-based biofuels.

The lesson that emerges from global experience is not that ethanol is bad. It is that the maximum scale to which crop-based ethanol can be taken is limited by land and water availability, and that each country has a different ceiling based on its specific geography, population density, and water resources.

"Brazil has 30 times more freshwater per person than India, less than half India's population, and nearly three times India's agricultural land area. The Amazon paradox

Brazil's agricultural land is vast partly because deforestation of the Amazon has opened up farmland over decades — a path India does not have. India has already converted most arable land to agriculture. There is very limited frontier land available for new crop expansion without displacing existing food crops or forest cover.

São Paulo vs the Indo-Gangetic Plain

Brazil's dominant sugarcane growing region — the state of São Paulo and surrounding areas — sits in a humid subtropical zone with abundant rainfall averaging 1,200-1,500mm annually, well suited to sugarcane. India's major sugarcane regions — parts of Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra's Marathwada, and Karnataka's Deccan plateau — frequently receive less than 1,000mm of rain, creating chronic groundwater dependency.

The scale gap nobody mentions

India's E85 target is 3× higher than Brazil's actual ethanol blend (27%). India wants to achieve what even Brazil has not attempted — with 6.7 times more people, less than half the land, and 30 times less freshwater per capita. The aspiration is not wrong, but the pathway to it must be fundamentally different from what Brazil did.

Chapter 08 · The Agricultural Dilemma

Food vs Fuel: The Farmer's Real Dilemma

The "food versus fuel" debate is often framed as a philosophical conflict. In reality, it is a straightforward economic and agricultural land management challenge that operates at the level of individual farmer decisions.

How a farmer makes the crop decision

Imagine a farmer in Uttar Pradesh with 5 acres of irrigated land. Every season, she can grow:

Given these economics, the rational farmer shifts toward sugarcane or maize. This is not greed — it is survival. The problem is that when millions of farmers make this rational individual choice simultaneously, the aggregate effect is a reduction in pulse and vegetable cultivation, which then creates food supply shortages and price increases for consumers.

The pulse shortage: a real example

India already faces chronic pulse (dal) shortages. The country imports approximately 2-4 million tonnes of pulses annually to meet domestic demand. Pulses are a critical protein source for hundreds of millions of vegetarian Indians. Yet pulses consistently receive less land because their profitability is lower than alternative crops. If E85 further incentivises maize and sugarcane over pulses, the protein gap could worsen significantly.

The benefits to farmers are real — and must be acknowledged

It would be unfair to present only the negative picture. The ethanol programme has delivered genuine and measurable benefits to rural India:

Real benefits of the ethanol programme for farmers

Price support: Ethanol demand creates a guaranteed buyer (OMCs) at government-set prices, providing income certainty that open markets often fail to deliver.

Distillery investment: Over 499 active distilleries create rural employment, infrastructure, and local economic activity.

Surplus utilisation: FCI rice stocks that would otherwise rot in government warehouses are converted into productive ethanol — eliminating procurement waste.

Maize revolution: Bihar's maize-based ethanol boom has created a new cash crop economy in a historically agrarian-stressed state.

The policy challenge is designing an ethanol programme that captures these farmer benefits without crossing the threshold where food security, water security, and land competition become unacceptable trade-offs. E20 arguably sits on the right side of that line. Whether E85 does is the central question of this blog.

Chapter 09 · The Export Dimension

India's Export Economy and the Concept of Virtual Water

India is one of the world's largest agricultural exporters. Every year, Indian farmers and processors send billions of dollars worth of food products to countries around the world. This is both an economic achievement and, from a water perspective, a complex strategic question.

What is virtual water?

When India exports a kilogram of rice, it is not just sending food — it is sending water. All the water used to grow that rice leaves India's borders permanently. Economists and water resource specialists call this virtual water export. The country that imports the rice gets not just calories but also the water that was embedded in producing those calories.

"India is simultaneously the world's largest extractor of groundwater AND one of the world's largest exporters of water-intensive agricultural commodities. Before adding large-scale ethanol crops to this system, we need to ask: is exporting water in the form of biofuel better or worse than exporting it in the form of rice?"

The strategic water accounting question

Here is the uncomfortable arithmetic: India exports roughly 85-100 billion cubic metres of virtual water annually through agricultural exports. It extracts 241 billion cubic metres of groundwater per year. The difference — approximately 140-155 billion cubic metres — supports domestic food, industrial and city water needs. Adding E85-scale ethanol production would add tens of billions of cubic metres to this demand, potentially pushing an already stressed system toward crisis.

Water competition becomes inevitable

Consider all the competing demands on India's water resources in the 2025-2040 timeframe:

Domestic food production Agricultural exports Ethanol / biofuels Industry & manufacturing Urban drinking water Semiconductor fabs Data centre cooling Hydroelectric power Climate-stressed ecosystems

Each of these is a legitimate need. The question is whether India has enough water to meet all of them simultaneously, or whether it must make hard choices about prioritisation.

The infrastructure problem: It is not just about ethanol production

Experts who have studied India's E85 infrastructure challenge point out that it is not merely a matter of producing more ethanol. The same petroleum infrastructure used for E20 cannot simply be repurposed for E85. Ethanol at high concentrations is corrosive to standard pipelines, storage tanks, and dispensing equipment. Moving to E85 would require replacing petrol dispensing units at every fuel station, upgrading all underground storage tanks, building separate ethanol-dedicated transport networks, and mandating flex-fuel vehicles across the entire car fleet. Industry sources estimate this infrastructure investment alone could run to several lakh crore rupees over a decade.

The seasonal supply challenge

India's current ethanol production depends heavily on molasses, which is seasonal and tied to the sugar crushing season (October to March). For higher blending levels, a year-round supply of grain-based ethanol from maize and rice is required. But grain supplies are also variable — a drought, a monsoon failure, or a pest outbreak can slash production. A country deeply dependent on E85 would be highly vulnerable to supply disruptions every time India has a bad agricultural year.

Chapter 11 · The Economics of Fuel

The Efficiency Problem Nobody Talks About: Cost per Kilometre, Not Cost per Litre

Proponents of E85 often highlight that ethanol-blended fuel is cheaper per litre than pure petrol. This is frequently true at the pump. But it is also one of the most misleading comparisons in energy policy, because it ignores a fundamental physical difference between ethanol and petrol: their energy content.

A concrete example: the household cost calculation

Suppose petrol costs ₹104/litre and E85 costs ₹85/litre (a 18% saving). A car using petrol at 15 km/L spends: 1,000 litres × ₹104 = ₹1,04,000 annually on fuel. The same car on E85 gets 10.5 km/L and spends: 1,430 litres × ₹85 = ₹1,21,550 annually. The driver who switched to the cheaper fuel (per litre) actually spent ₹17,550 more per year. This is the efficiency trap of comparing biofuels on a per-litre basis rather than a per-kilometre basis.

Does this mean E85 is always more expensive to use?

Not necessarily. If ethanol prices are sufficiently discounted — as they sometimes are in Brazil, where ethanol is the domestic product and petrol is imported — E85 can still be cost-competitive on a per-kilometre basis. The key threshold is: E85 is cost-competitive if its price per litre is less than 70% of petrol's price per litre. India's current ethanol procurement price of ₹71.55 per litre (which is actually higher than refined petrol cost in some quarters) does not currently meet this threshold.

Higher octane — the real technical advantage

Ethanol has a significantly higher octane rating (Research Octane Number of 108-113) compared to standard petrol (~91-95 RON). In specially optimised engines (higher compression ratio, adjusted timing), this allows for improved thermal efficiency that can partially offset the lower energy density. However, this benefit is only captured in purpose-built or significantly modified engines — not in the standard fleet running on E20.

Chapter 12 · The Strategic Choice

Water, AI, Semiconductors — The New Water Economy

The final dimension of the E85 debate is the most forward-looking and the most important for India's long-term trajectory. Water is not just an agricultural input. It is rapidly becoming a strategic industrial resource — and India's ability to compete in the 21st-century economy may depend on how it allocates its groundwater.

AI and data centres: the hidden water consumers

Most people think artificial intelligence runs on electricity. It does — but it also runs on water. Data centres generate enormous heat as servers process information. Cooling these data centres is typically achieved through water-based cooling systems. A large hyperscale data centre can consume millions of litres of water per day.

Semiconductor fabrication: the most water-intensive industry

Semiconductor chip manufacturing — making the processors that power everything from smartphones to defence systems to AI chips — is extraordinarily water-intensive. A single semiconductor fab (fabrication plant) can consume 15-20 million litres of ultrapure water per day. This water must be treated to extraordinary purity levels, used in chip washing processes, and then treated again before disposal.

India's National Semiconductor Mission aims to establish multiple semiconductor fabs by 2030. Tata Electronics is building a fab in Assam; another is planned in Gujarat. These facilities will need reliable, clean water supplies. If the groundwater table in those regions is depleted by surrounding agricultural ethanol crops, the fabs either cannot operate or must invest enormously in water treatment and recycling.

Solar manufacturing vs ethanol: the sunlight comparison

Both ethanol and solar energy can be thought of as ways of capturing sunlight. But they do it very differently in terms of efficiency and water consumption:

Solar photovoltaic panels convert 18-22% of sunlight into electricity. Sugarcane converts approximately 0.3% of sunlight into chemical energy in the form of sucrose. On a land-area basis, solar PV produces 50-70 times more usable energy than ethanol from the same piece of land. With almost zero water consumption compared to agricultural ethanol's thousands of litres per litre of fuel equivalent.

"The question is not simply whether India can produce E85 — it is whether dedicating land and water to crop-based ethanol is the highest-value use of those resources in an era of cheap solar power, EV batteries, and a global semiconductor race."

The capital allocation question

Suppose the Indian government and private sector invest ₹2 lakh crore over 10 years to scale from E20 to E85. What are the alternatives for that same capital?

Option A: E85 Scale-Up

  • Reduce crude oil imports by ~1.5-2 billion litres/year
  • Save ~$15-20 billion in annual forex (at current prices)
  • Support farmer incomes in cane/maize belt
  • Risk: water stress, food competition, supply variability

Option B: Solar + Battery

  • Add 80-100 GW of solar manufacturing capacity
  • Displace petrol via EV charging on clean grid
  • Create export-oriented solar panel industry
  • No water consumption for energy production

Option C: Semiconductor Fabs

  • 1-2 leading-edge or 3-4 lagging-edge fabs
  • Establish India in global chip supply chain
  • Enable AI, defence, EV ecosystem self-reliance
  • High technology transfer and IP development

Option D: 2G Ethanol + EVs

  • Scale second-generation (residue-based) ethanol
  • Get ethanol benefits without food/water trade-offs
  • Simultaneously build EV charging infrastructure
  • Balanced transition without choosing a single bet

India need not choose just one of these options. But the resource constraints of land, water, capital, and policy attention mean that the more aggressively India pursues E85, the less capacity it has to pursue the other paths. These are real opportunity costs, not abstract ones.

Conclusion

Connecting the Dots: The Real Debate

The E85 debate, examined in its full complexity, is not a fuel debate. It is simultaneously a water policy, an agricultural policy, a farmer income policy, an export trade policy, a technology investment policy, and a decision about what kind of economy India wants to build for the next 25 years.

India's achievement of E20 blending nationwide — five years ahead of schedule — is a genuine policy success story. It demonstrates what determined government action can achieve in energy transitions. The ethanol programme has saved foreign exchange, supported farmer incomes, created rural distillery jobs, and utilised surplus grain stocks. These are real achievements that deserve acknowledgment.

But going from E20 to E85 is not the same kind of challenge. It is a fundamentally different order of magnitude — requiring 3 to 4 times more ethanol production, with all the associated land, water, infrastructure, and farmer crop-choice implications that multiply proportionally.

The central finding of this analysis is that India faces a genuine water constraint that makes E85 at full scale deeply problematic if pursued through conventional food crop-based ethanol routes. With 90% of groundwater extraction already going to agriculture, and multiple states already in severe overdraft, the additional water demand of E85-scale maize or sugarcane cultivation could accelerate a groundwater crisis with consequences for food production, urban water supply, and industrial development alike.

The most intelligent path forward appears to be a combination: maintain E20 as the national petrol standard (already achieved), aggressively scale second-generation ethanol from agricultural waste, pursue EV electrification powered by solar, and direct major capital investment toward semiconductors, battery manufacturing, and solar panel production — industries that convert sunlight to energy with 50-70 times greater efficiency than crop-based ethanol, with minimal water consumption.

The goal of reducing India's dependence on imported crude oil is correct and important. The question is whether crop-based E85 is the right instrument to achieve it — or whether the same capital, land, and water invested differently could achieve greater energy security, greater farmer prosperity, and a stronger competitive position in the technology economy of the 21st century.

"Should India convert sunlight into crops and then crops into fuel — or convert sunlight directly into electricity, batteries, semiconductors, and advanced manufacturing? That is the real E85 debate. And now you know why it matters."
